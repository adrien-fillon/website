abstract: Self-interest is a central driver of attitudes and behaviors, but people also act against their ‚Äéimmediate self-interest through prosocial behaviors, voting incongruously with their finances, ‚Äéor punishing others at personal cost. How much people believe that self-interest causes ‚Äéattitudes and behaviors is important, because this belief may shape regulation, shared ‚Äénarratives, and institutional structures. An influential paper claimed that people overestimate ‚Äéthe power of self-interest on others' attitudes and behavioral intentions (Miller & Ratner, ‚Äé‚Äé1998). We present two registered, close, and successful replications (U.S. MTurk, N = 800; ‚ÄéU.K. Prolific, N = 799) that compared actual to estimated intentions, with open data and code. ‚ÄéConsistent with the original article, participants overestimated the impact of payment on blood ‚Äédonation in Study 1, ds = 0.59 [0.51, 0.66], 0.57 [0.49, 0.64], and overestimated the importance ‚Äéof smoking status for smoking policy preferences in Study 4, ds = 0.75 [0.59, 0.90], 0.84 [0.73, ‚Äé‚Äé0.96]. These replications included two extensions 1) communal orientation as a moderator of ‚Äéoverestimation and 2) a more detailed measure of self-interest in Study 4 (ordinal smoking ‚Äéstatus). Communal orientation did not predict overestimation, and the ordinal smoking measure ‚Äéyielded similar results to the main study. Verifying the overestimation error informs behavioral ‚Äétheories across several fields and has practical implications for institutions that require trust ‚Äéand cooperation.
